\begin{abstract}
Interactive AI agents that operate through remote shells are sensitive to transport-layer latency in their action-observation loops, yet no prior study systematically compares the protocols available for this task. We present a controlled measurement study of three remote shell protocols---SSHv2, SSH3 (HTTP/3 over QUIC), and Mosh (UDP with state synchronization)---under network conditions ranging from near-ideal to severely impaired (10--100\,ms one-way delay, 0--3\% loss). Using a pexpect-based benchmark framework, we evaluate command completion latency and output completeness for workloads representative of AI agent interactions. Our results reveal three key findings: (1)~SSH3 achieves 2.2--3.4$\times$ faster session establishment than SSHv2 owing to QUIC's 1-RTT handshake; (2)~for bulk-output commands, SSH3's userspace QUIC implementation is 1.7--2.2$\times$ slower than SSHv2's kernel TCP, despite delivering more complete output; (3)~Mosh delivers only 2--6\% of expected output for large commands due to its frame-skipping design, making it unsuitable for AI agents that must parse command output. We derive practical guidelines for selecting remote control protocols based on workload characteristics.
\end{abstract}
