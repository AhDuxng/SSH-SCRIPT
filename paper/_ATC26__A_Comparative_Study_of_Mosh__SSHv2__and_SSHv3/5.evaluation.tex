\subsection{Session Setup Latency}
\label{subsec:session-setup}

We measure session setup as the time from client process spawn to the first interactive shell prompt in Section~\ref{subsec:measurement-metrics}, pooled across all sessions of each (protocol, scenario) configuration (N = 400).

Figure~\ref{fig:session-setup} shows that SSH3 achieves the lowest setup latency in every scenario. In the VPN scenario it establishes a session in 219\,ms, versus 765\,ms for SSHv2 and 908\,ms for Mosh. The same ordering holds under impairment, reaching 1042\,ms vs.\ 3565\,ms and 3856\,ms in the High scenario. This follows from protocol design: SSH3 uses QUIC's 1-RTT handshake, which folds transport setup and key exchange into one round trip, whereas SSHv2 needs a TCP handshake plus SSH key exchange and authentication. Mosh incurs the highest setup cost because it first completes a full SSHv2 session to bootstrap its UDP-based SSP channel.

\subsection{Workload Latency Results}

\paragraph{W1}
We first evaluate the completion latency for four short output commands. It is shown that SSH3 achieves the best performance across all four network scenarios. For example, in the VPN scenario, SSH3 offers an average latency of 81.6\,ms, marking a 12\% and 16\% reduction over its SSHv2 and Mosh counterparts (which are 93.1\,ms and 97.0\,ms, respectively). More interestingly, the performance gap between SSH3 and the baselines widens remarkably under degraded network conditions. Specifically, under the High scenario, SSH3 requires only 292.9\,ms, while those of Mosh and SSHv2 are 324.4\,ms and 384.4\,ms. This reflects how each transport handles loss once a session is established. Since each command produces under one kilobyte, a single request-response round trip dominates the latency. Under degraded network conditions, SSHv2 suffers from TCP HOL stalls, whereas SSH3 shows lower observed recovery delay, consistent with QUIC's userspace loss recovery. On the other hand, Mosh demonstrates no advantage in W1 under VPN and Low scenarios because its SSP protocol has little opportunity to coalesce redundant updates for sub-kilobyte responses. While Mosh's local loss recovery helps it overtake SSHv2 under the High scenario, SSH3 still outperforms all its counterparts.

\paragraph{W2}
Next, we compare the completion latency for large output commands. Unlike W1, no single protocol dominates across all network conditions. SSH3 achieves the best performance over VPN with 87.7\,ms, while that of SSHv2 is 159.8\,ms. However, SSHv2 is marginally better under the Low scenario with 432.0\,ms, and Mosh offers the lowest latency under Medium and High scenarios (561.4\,ms and 709.6\,ms, respectively). Notably, SSHv2 experiences the most severe degradation, with its latency surging to 2680.0\,ms under the High scenario. It should be noted that Mosh's remarkably low latency under impairment must be interpreted alongside its output completeness. As demonstrated in Section~\ref{subsec:output-completeness}, Mosh receives only a small fraction of the expected bytes for heavy output, indicating that its SSP synchronizes screen state rather than the byte stream. This observation indicates that Mosh's W2 latency merely measures when the terminal becomes responsive again, rather than when the complete output arrives. This confirms that W2 trades apparent responsiveness against completeness: while Mosh can appear faster, only SSHv2 and SSH3 deliver the full output required by agents parsing large results. Table~\ref{tab:tail-p95} reports p95 latency under High impairment. The effect is most severe for SSHv2 on large output: its W2 p95 exceeds 8\,s against a 2.68\,s mean, reflecting the heavy tail that TCP HOL stalls produce under loss and that agents must absorb through generous timeouts.

\paragraph{W3}
Finally, we consider the keystroke-to-echo latency in both single-pane and five-pane tmux layouts. It is shown that Mosh achieves the best performance in every scenario and layout, especially under degraded network conditions. For instance, under the High scenario, Mosh maintains a latency of 25.3-31.3\,ms, which is significantly lower than that of SSHv2 and SSH3 (both exceeding 220\,ms). This gap is driven by Mosh's local echo and predictive rendering, which hide part of the network delay. Furthermore, when comparing SSH3 and SSHv2, SSH3 improves the latency over VPN (achieving 2.1\,ms vs.\ 20.2\,ms in the single-pane layout, and 9.4\,ms vs.\ 23.9\,ms in the five-pane layout). However, under the Low scenario, SSH3 becomes slightly worse than its SSHv2 counterpart for both layouts (e.g., 29.3\,ms vs.\ 26.1\,ms for single-pane). This indicates that QUIC does not substantially reduce steady-state keystroke latency once network delay dominates the per-character path. Besides, moving from one to five panes significantly slows down SSHv2 and SSH3 under impairment due to increased network-crossing screen updates, whereas Mosh is largely unaffected. This is consistent with Mosh's update coalescing and local prediction, making it the most responsive protocol for interactive workloads.

\label{subsec:output-completeness}

Output completeness measures whether the client receives the expected output. Figure~\ref{fig:recv-pct} reports the mean percentage of received bytes, comparing captured output against a no-PTY reference collected through direct SSH execution. SSHv2 and SSH3 stay near-complete: about 99\% of expected bytes for small-output commands and above 95\% for large output under all impaired scenarios, showing that byte-stream semantics suit workloads that require complete output. Mosh differs sharply: it receives 89-92\% of bytes for small-output commands (decreasing with network impairment) but only 2-6\% for large output. This matches SSP's design, which synchronizes the visible screen state rather than every byte, so when updates arrive faster than the frame interval, intermediate states are skipped and only the latest screen is sent.

This is acceptable for human use, where the final visible screen matters more than byte-level completeness, but problematic for agents, which parse output to read file listings, process lists, errors, or system state: skipped output can make the agent act on incomplete data. Hence Mosh offers the best responsiveness, but SSHv2 and SSH3 are the reliable choice when completeness is required.


\subsection{Key Takeaway}
\label{subsec:eval-summary}

Across workloads, no single protocol wins (RQ1, RQ4): SSH3 gives the lowest setup and small-output latency, Mosh the lowest keystroke latency, and SSHv2/SSH3 the most complete large output (RQ2). SSH3 improves on SSHv2 chiefly via faster setup and loss recovery (RQ3). Selection should follow the dominant workload: SSH3 for command-oriented loops, Mosh for latency-sensitive editing that tolerates occasional output loss.
